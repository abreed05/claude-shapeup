# Scope Hammering

*Used by the (planned) `shapeup:scope-hammer` skill for mid-cycle scope cuts.*

**Scope hammering** is Shape Up's mid-cycle response to "this bet won't fit the appetite." The answer is always **cut scope**, never **extend time**.

## Why cut, not extend

Extending time invalidates the appetite. The appetite was a decision about how much the problem is worth — if you spend more, you're making a new bet without betting-table approval. Cutting scope keeps the original bet intact: you still ship within the cycle, just with a smaller surface.

## When to scope-hammer

Signals that a bet needs hammering:

- A scope has been sitting on the uphill side of the hill chart for weeks.
- Integration surprises are eating the cycle.
- The team realizes a rabbit hole is deeper than shaped.
- Halfway through the cycle, the remaining work looks bigger than the remaining time.

Don't wait until the last week. The earlier you hammer, the more choices you have.

## How to hammer

Ask these questions, in order:

1. **What is the minimum shippable surface?** The smallest thing that delivers the core value of the bet. Everything beyond that is optional.
2. **What can become a no-go?** Anything you'd reasonably include but don't strictly need. Promote it from "scope" to "no-go" and move on.
3. **What scopes can be deferred to a future bet?** If something is worth doing but not essential, it's a future pitch, not a forced inclusion.
4. **What can be done more simply?** A cheap version of a feature is often 80% of the value at 20% of the cost.
5. **Is the bet itself wrong?** Sometimes hammering reveals that the pitch was mis-shaped. In rare cases, the right call is to drop the bet entirely.

## What scope-hammering is NOT

- **Not reducing quality.** Cut features, not rigor. Polished-small is the target, not sloppy-big.
- **Not an estimate refresh.** You're not re-predicting how long the original scope takes; you're deciding what to leave out.
- **Not an extension in disguise.** "We'll hammer by pushing the last scope to cool-down" is extending, not hammering.

## Aftermath

A hammered bet ships with less than originally pitched. That's fine. The retro captures what got cut and why; the shaping team feeds that into future pitches so next time's rabbit holes reflect this cycle's surprises.

## Connection to shaping

Pitches with strong **no-gos sections** are easier to hammer — the discipline of deciding what you're NOT doing transfers directly to mid-cycle cutting. Weak no-gos → scope creep → harder hammering. See `references/no-gos.md`.
