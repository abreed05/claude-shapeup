# Appetite

**Appetite is the primary Shape Up discipline.** Every pitch declares it. It's the most important difference between Shape Up and estimation-based methodologies.

## Definition

Appetite is **the time we're willing to spend** on a problem — not an estimate of what it'll take. It's a cap, not a prediction. If the work doesn't fit the appetite, the response is to **cut scope**, not extend time.

## Standard sizes

- **Small batch — 1 to 2 weeks.** Focused, constrained problems. Multiple small batches can fit in a cycle, but each should be individually scoped.
- **Big batch — 6 weeks.** The standard Shape Up cycle. A meaningful bet that one team takes for the full cycle.

Anything else is **non-standard** and requires written justification visible to the betting table.

## Appetite vs estimate — why it matters

Estimates are predictions — they answer "how long will this take?" Appetite is a decision — it answers "how much is this worth?" The difference changes what happens when work runs long:

- **Estimate-based:** you missed the estimate; extend the deadline or re-plan.
- **Appetite-based:** you ran out of appetite; cut scope to fit what's left, or drop the bet entirely.

This is why the circuit breaker works. The cap is the cap.

## Common violations

- **"A few weeks"** — not an appetite. Either small or big batch, or a specific number with justification.
- **"As long as it takes"** — the opposite of shape up. No appetite = no bet.
- **Hiding an estimate inside an appetite** — "we think 3 weeks" is an estimate; "we're willing to spend 2 weeks, and here's what we'll cut if it doesn't fit" is an appetite.

## When to pick small vs big

- Small batch: the problem is narrow, the solution is obvious in outline, and a longer bet would invite scope creep.
- Big batch: the problem is meaningful enough to justify a team's full cycle, and the risks justify the shaping investment.

If you can't decide, you probably haven't shaped the problem enough. Revisit `references/pitches.md`.
